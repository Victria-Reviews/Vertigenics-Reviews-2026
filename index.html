Vertigenics Ingredients: What Is Inside Each Capsule

When assessing is vertigenics any good, the most objective starting point is the vertigenics ingredients list. Each compound in the formula should have either clinical research or mechanistic rationale supporting its relevance to vestibular and inner ear health. Below is a breakdown of the key ingredients and what the available evidence suggests about their function.

Ginkgo Biloba Extract
Vestibular Circulation

Ginkgo biloba is one of the most studied botanical compounds for inner ear circulation. Research suggests it enhances blood flow to the cochlear and vestibular arteries by reducing platelet aggregation and improving vascular tone. Clinical evidence indicates meaningful benefit for dizziness and tinnitus when used consistently over 8 to 12 weeks.

Vitamin B6 (Pyridoxine)
Nausea and Nerve Support

Vitamin B6 has well-established antiemetic properties and is used clinically for nausea associated with vestibular disorders. Beyond nausea control, B6 plays a critical role in neurotransmitter synthesis within vestibular neural pathways. Studies show that deficiency in B6 is associated with increased susceptibility to motion-related dizziness.

Magnesium Glycinate
Inner Ear Fluid Balance

Magnesium supports the regulation of ion transport in the endolymph fluid of the cochlea, which is central to normal hearing and balance signaling. Research suggests magnesium deficiency is more prevalent in people with Meniere's disease and chronic tinnitus. Glycinate form provides superior absorption with minimal digestive side effects compared to oxide forms.

Vinpocetine
Cerebrovascular Support

Vinpocetine is a synthetic compound derived from the periwinkle plant that has been studied for its ability to improve cerebral blood flow and reduce neurological inflammation. Clinical evidence from European research indicates it may reduce the frequency of vertigo episodes in patients with cerebrovascular-related balance disorders by improving oxygen delivery to brainstem and vestibular processing centers.

Lemon Bioflavonoids
Endolymph Regulation

Lemon bioflavonoids, particularly hesperidin and rutin, have been used specifically for Meniere's disease-associated vertigo in clinical settings. Research suggests these compounds help reduce the accumulation of excess endolymph fluid in the cochlear canal, which is the primary mechanism behind Meniere's-type spinning episodes. Studies show they are best effective when used as part of a long-term supplementation protocol.

Vitamin B12 (Methylcobalamin)
Vestibular Nerve Health

B12 in the methylcobalamin form is essential for maintaining the myelin sheath that insulates the vestibular nerve fibers responsible for balance signal transmission. Research indicates that B12 deficiency, which is common in adults over 50, is a significant and underdiagnosed contributor to balance disorders and dizziness. Supplementation has been shown to improve vestibular symptoms in deficient individuals within 8 to 12 weeks.

Alpha Lipoic Acid
Cochlear Antioxidant

Alpha lipoic acid (ALA) provides antioxidant protection to the cochlear hair cells, which are highly vulnerable to oxidative damage from noise exposure, inflammation, and age-related metabolic changes. Studies show that ALA supplementation can reduce noise-induced hearing loss and may help protect residual inner ear function in adults with established vestibular damage. It works in both water and fat environments, giving broad cellular coverage.

Zinc (as Zinc Picolinate)
Inner Ear Immunity

Zinc plays a specific role in inner ear immune defense and cochlear function. Research suggests that zinc deficiency increases susceptibility to viral labyrinthitis, a common trigger of sudden-onset vertigo. The picolinate form provides high bioavailability, and supplementation has been associated with reduced severity of tinnitus and improved recovery from vestibular neuritis in clinical observations.
